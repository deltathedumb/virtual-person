Personal hygiene routine.

A basic daily routine includes washing the face and hands, brushing teeth at
least twice a day, showering or bathing regularly, and wearing clean clothes.
Precondition: water, soap, and a clean towel are available. Steps for
showering: adjust water to a comfortable temperature before stepping in, wash
with soap starting from the top of the body downward, rinse thoroughly, turn
off the water, and dry off before dressing. Success condition: skin and hair
are clean and the person feels refreshed rather than still uncomfortable.

Failure case: if the water heater is not working and only cold water is
available, a person may choose to wait, use a different bathroom if one
exists, or accept a quick cold wash if hygiene need is urgent; this is a
judgment call based on how uncomfortable waiting would be versus how
unpleasant the cold water is.

---

Restroom routines.

Using the restroom promptly when bladder pressure is noticeable, rather than
delaying repeatedly, avoids discomfort and possible accidents. After using
the toilet, flush, wash hands with soap for a reasonable amount of time, and
dry them. If the toilet does not flush properly or continues running
afterward, that is worth reporting or addressing rather than ignoring
repeatedly, since it wastes water and may indicate a developing problem.

---

Laundry.

Steps: sort clothes by color and fabric type where practical, load the
washing machine without overfilling it, add an appropriate amount of
detergent, select a cycle matched to the fabric, and start the machine.
Precondition: the machine is available and not already in use by someone
else's load. When the cycle finishes, move clothes to the dryer promptly or
hang them to air dry, since leaving wet clothes sitting too long can cause a
musty smell requiring a rewash. Once dry, fold or hang clothes and put them
away rather than leaving them in a pile indefinitely.

Failure case: if a colored item is accidentally washed with whites and causes
discoloration, note which item caused it, separate that item in the future,
and treat the affected clothing with an appropriate stain approach or accept
the discoloration if it cannot be corrected.

---

Cleaning and household organization.

Regular light cleaning, such as wiping counters, sweeping floors, and putting
items back in their designated places after use, prevents small messes from
becoming large ones. When cleaning with any chemical product, read the label
first, avoid mixing different cleaning chemicals unless the label specifically
allows it, since some combinations produce dangerous fumes, and ventilate the
room if the product has a strong smell.

Organizing a household benefits from consistent places for commonly used
items, such as keys, chargers, and important documents, so that time is not
wasted searching. A short routine of returning items to their place after use
is more effective than periodic large reorganizations alone.
