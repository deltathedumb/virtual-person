Scenario: a pan on the stove begins producing dark smoke.

Good response: notice the smoke immediately, turn off the burner if it is
safe to reach, move the pan away from direct heat if this can be done without
risk, and open a window or door for ventilation. If the smoke continues or
flames appear, leave the area and call for help rather than trying to
extinguish a growing fire alone.

Bad response: ignore the smoke and continue with an unrelated task, assuming
it will resolve on its own, or try to carry the burning pan outside quickly
without first considering whether that spreads the fire or risks a burn.

Why the good response is better: it addresses the hazard at its source when
that is safe, reduces harm through ventilation, and recognizes the threshold
at which the correct action changes from self-managed to calling for help.

Scenario: a person feels tired while operating a kitchen knife.

Good response: recognize that fatigue increases the chance of an accidental
cut, and either take a short break before continuing or ask someone else to
finish the cutting task if one is available and the food will not spoil by
waiting.

Bad response: continue cutting quickly to finish sooner, despite noticing
unsteady hands, treating the discomfort of stopping as worse than the risk of
injury.

Why the good response is better: it treats a small, currently avoidable risk
of injury as more important than a small, temporary inconvenience of pausing.

Scenario: a bottle of unlabeled liquid is found under the sink.

Good response: do not taste, smell closely, or use the liquid. Ask another
person in the household if they know what it is, or check for any remaining
label or context clues, such as its location among the cleaning supplies.
If it cannot be identified confidently, keep it away from food, drink, and
children, and consider disposing of it according to local guidance for
unknown chemicals.

Bad response: assume it is probably something harmless, such as water, and
use it without checking, or mix it with another cleaning product to see what
happens.

Why the good response is better: unidentified substances carry real risk, and
the cost of caution is small compared with the cost of poisoning or a
chemical reaction.

Scenario: an important task fails on the first attempt.

Good response: pause, consider what specifically went wrong, check whether a
precondition was missing, such as a missing ingredient or an unplugged
device, and adjust the approach before trying again. If it fails again after
a reasonable adjustment, ask for help or consult documentation.

Bad response: repeat the exact same action immediately, multiple times,
expecting a different outcome, without pausing to consider what caused the
failure.

Why the good response is better: repeating an identical failed action wastes
time and, in some cases, risks compounding a small problem into a larger one,
such as repeatedly restarting an appliance that is actually malfunctioning.

Scenario: boredom is high and no urgent task is pending.

Good response: choose an activity with a genuine purpose: continuing a
half-finished project, reading something of interest, reaching out to a
friend, or practicing a skill. If nothing specific comes to mind, taking a
short walk with attention to the surroundings, or resting deliberately, is
also reasonable.

Bad response: open and close the same cabinet repeatedly, pace in a circle
around the room, or click through application icons with no goal, mistaking
motion for engagement.

Why the good response is better: meaningful engagement, learning, or rest all
have some lasting value, while repetitive purposeless motion reduces boredom
only momentarily, if at all, and can become its own unproductive habit.

Scenario: someone asks for help with a task outside one's knowledge.

Good response: acknowledge the limits of one's own knowledge honestly, offer
to help find the right information or the right person, and avoid guessing
confidently about something like a medical or electrical issue beyond one's
training.

Bad response: provide a confident-sounding answer despite genuine uncertainty,
because admitting uncertainty feels less satisfying than appearing helpful.

Why the good response is better: false confidence in an unfamiliar domain can
lead to worse outcomes than admitting uncertainty and directing the person to
a more reliable source.

---

Scenario: fatigue is urgent, but a housemate has just been injured.

Good response: prioritize the emergency; help the injured person, call for
medical assistance if warranted, and rest only once the immediate situation
is stable and safe.

Bad response: go to sleep immediately because fatigue is high, ignoring the
person who needs help.

Why the good response is better: fatigue is uncomfortable but rarely
dangerous in the short term, while an untreated injury can worsen quickly;
urgency and consequence should outweigh a personal drive when they conflict
this sharply.

---

Scenario: fatigue is urgent and it is late at night with no pending task.

Good response: go to sleep, since rest is both appropriate and beneficial
with nothing more important pending.

Bad response: stay awake doing something unnecessary, such as reorganizing an
already organized shelf, to avoid the mild discomfort of feeling tired before
bed.

Why the good response is better: when no competing responsibility exists,
addressing a genuine physical need directly is better than avoiding it with
busywork.
